## Evidence Quality Guide

### Understanding the Scoring System

**A1 - Government/Legal Primary Sources**
- Best for: Legal arguments, policy work
- Examples: Statutes, court decisions, government reports
- Why strong: Official, authoritative, legally binding

**B1 - Official Data/Verified Statistics**
- Best for: Empirical claims, statistical arguments
- Examples: Government databases, FOIL data, audit reports
- Why strong: Verifiable, comprehensive, objective

**C1 - Credentialed Sources**
- Best for: Context, expert opinion, analysis
- Examples: Peer-reviewed research, established journalism
- Why strong: Professional standards, editorial process

**D1 - Advocacy with Verification**
- Best for: Supplementary evidence, lived experience
- Examples: NGO reports with FOIL data, documented investigations
- Why strong: Verified data, transparent methodology

### When Is Evidence "Good Enough"?

**For Academic Publication:**
- Aim for 60%+ A1/B1
- C1 acceptable for context
- All sources peer-review quality

**For Legal Briefing:**
- Aim for 75%+ A1/B1
- Must withstand opposing counsel
- All sources admissible

**For Policy Advocacy:**
- Aim for 70%+ A1/B1
- Must convince skeptics
- All sources verifiable

**For Public Communication:**
- Mix is acceptable
- Prioritize accessibility
- All sources traceable

## Best Practices

### Working with AI

**Do:**
- Answer questions thoroughly
- Provide context when available
- Question AI's assessments
- Verify extracted information
- Save work frequently

**Don't:**
- Rush through evidence
- Accept weak sources
- Skip documentation
- Ignore logical gaps
- Forget to verify

### Evidence Gathering

**Do:**
- Start with best available sources
- Extract systematically
- Document as you go
- Note limitations
- Track gaps

**Don't:**
- Use sources you can't verify
- Cherry-pick favorable evidence
- Ignore contradictions
- Skip quality assessment
- Forget source locations

### Documentation

**Do:**
- Document decisions immediately
- Explain reasoning
- Note alternatives considered
- Track dead ends
- Update logs regularly

**Don't:**
- Wait to document later
- Assume things are obvious
- Skip gap tracking
- Forget research process
- Leave placeholders
